<p>In a little district west of Washington Square the streets have run crazy. At the top of a squatty, three-story brick Sue and Johnsy had their studio. In November a cold, unseen stranger, whom the doctors called <span class="vocab-tip" data-tip="Pneumonia：肺炎">Pneumonia</span>, stalked about the colony, touching one here and there with his icy fingers.</p>
<p>Johnsy lay, scarcely moving, on her painted iron bedstead, looking through the window at the blank side of the next brick house. The doctor invited Sue into the hallway. "She has one chance in—let us say, ten," he said. "And that chance is for her to want to live. Your little lady has made up her mind that she's not going to get well."</p>
<p>Johnsy's eyes were open wide. She was looking out the window and counting backward. "Twelve," she said, and a little later "eleven"; and then "ten," and "nine"; and then "eight" and "seven". An old, old ivy vine, <span class="vocab-tip" data-tip="gnarled：粗糙多節的、老態龍鍾的">gnarled</span> and decayed at the roots, climbed half way up the brick wall. "What is it, dear?" asked Sue. "Six," said Johnsy, in almost a whisper. "They are falling faster now. When the last one falls I must go, too."</p>

<p>Old Behrman was a painter who lived on the ground floor beneath them. He was past sixty and he was a failure in art. For forty years he had wielded the brush without success. He had been always about to paint a masterpiece, but had never yet begun it. Sue told Behrman of Johnsy's fancy.</p>
<p>When Sue awoke from an hour's sleep the next morning she found Johnsy staring at the window. "Pull it up; I want to see," she ordered. Wearily Sue obeyed. But, lo! after the beating rain and fierce gusts of wind that had endured through the livelong night, there yet stood out against the brick wall one ivy leaf. It was the last one on the vine. Still dark green near its stem, it hung bravely from a branch.</p>
<p>"It is the last one," said Johnsy. "I thought it would surely fall during the night. It will fall today, and I shall die at the same time." The day wore away, and even through the twilight they could see the lone ivy leaf clinging to its stem against the wall. The next morning the leaf was still there. Johnsy lay for a long time looking at it. "I've been a bad girl, Sudie," said Johnsy. "Something has made that last leaf stay there to show me how wicked I was. It is a sin to want to die."</p>
<p>The doctor came in the afternoon. "She's out of danger. You've won." And that afternoon Sue came to the bed. "I have something to tell you," she said. "Mr. Behrman died of pneumonia today in the hospital. He was ill only two days. The janitor found him helpless with pain. His shoes and clothing were wet through and icy cold. They couldn't imagine where he had been on such a dreadful night."</p>
<p>"And then they found a lantern, still lighted, and a ladder, and some scattered brushes, and a palette with green and yellow colors mixed on it, and—look out the window, dear, at the last ivy leaf on the wall. Didn't you wonder why it never fluttered or moved when the wind blew? Oh, darling, it's Behrman's masterpiece—he painted it there the night that the last leaf fell."</p>
