<p>One afternoon I was sitting outside the Cafe de la Paix, watching the <span class="vocab-tip" data-tip="splendour：輝煌、華麗">splendour</span> and squalor of Parisian life, when I heard some one call my name. I turned round, and saw Lord Murchison. We had not met since we had been at Oxford together, nearly ten years before, so I was delighted to come across him again. We shook hands warmly, and decided to dine together.</p>
<p>I noticed that Murchison was changed. He looked anxious and <span class="vocab-tip" data-tip="perplexed：困惑的、複雜的">perplexed</span>, and seemed to be in a state of profound doubt. I felt that some mystery was overshadowing his life. "What is the matter?" I asked. "You look as if you had a secret." "I am terribly puzzled," he said. "I want your advice. I have met a woman, and I loved her. We were engaged to be married. And yet, she is gone, and I am left in utter darkness."</p>
<p>"Tell me the whole story," I replied. He took a small leather case from his pocket and handed it to me. Inside was the photograph of a woman. She was beautiful, but her expression was strangely mysterious. Her eyes seemed to hold a deep riddle, like the Sphinx. "Who is she?" I asked. "Her name is Lady Alroy," he said. "She always wore a veil, she never gave a direct answer, and she seemed to live in a world of constant evasion."</p>
<blockquote>
    "She was a Sphinx without a secret."<br>
    <span style="font-size:14px; font-style:normal; color:#8c8a88;">（她是一個沒有秘密的斯芬克斯［謎一樣的女人］。）</span>
</blockquote>
<p>"One day," Murchison continued, "I followed her. She went to a quiet, shabby street in a poor quarter of London. She entered a small lodging-house, using a key of her own. She stayed there for two hours. I was filled with jealousy and suspicion. The next day, I went to the house and spoke to the landlady. The landlady told me it was rented by Lady Alroy, but that she never slept there—she only came occasionally, sat in the room for an hour or two, read a book, drank a cup of tea, and left."</p>
<p>"I confronted her that evening," Murchison said, his voice trembling. "I broke off our engagement in a fit of anger. A week later, she caught a terrible cold, and within days, she died of pneumonia." "My dear Murchison," I said, "the answer is simple. She rented that room merely for the pleasure of being mysterious. She loved the romance of secrecy, the thrill of having a hidden life, even though there was absolutely nothing to hide. She was a Sphinx without a secret."</p>
